About Atmams Temple

Our Sacred History

Founded in 1850, Atmams Temple has been a beacon of spiritual enlightenment for generations. Our temple was established by the revered saint Shri Atmamanda Saraswati to preserve the ancient Vedic traditions and provide a sacred space for devotees to connect with the divine.

The temple architecture follows the traditional Kerala style, with intricate wood carvings and copper-plated roofs that reflect our rich cultural heritage.

Spiritual Significance

Atmams Temple is dedicated to Lord Shiva as the primary deity, with shrines for Lord Ganesha, Goddess Parvati, and Lord Murugan. The temple is renowned for its powerful energy field created through centuries of continuous worship and rituals.

Our main deity is the ancient Shiva Lingam, believed to be self-manifested (Swayambhu), which makes it particularly sacred and powerful for meditation and worship.

Daily Rituals

The temple follows a strict schedule of daily rituals:

- 5:00 AM: Temple opening with Suprabhatam
- 6:00 AM: Morning Abhishekam
- 12:00 PM: Midday Pooja
- 6:00 PM: Evening Deeparadhana
- 8:00 PM: Temple closing with Arthi

Special rituals are performed on full moon days, new moon days, and all important Hindu festivals.

Our Revered Founder

Shri Atmamanda Saraswati

Founder & Spiritual Guide (1820-1895)

Shri Atmamanda Saraswati was a renowned spiritual master and scholar born in the village of Devipuram in Kerala. From a young age, he exhibited extraordinary spiritual inclinations and profound wisdom that attracted seekers from all walks of life.

After years of intense meditation and study of the Vedas under the guidance of his guru, Shri Atmamanda Saraswati established the Atmams Temple in 1850 with the vision of creating a sacred space where devotees could experience the divine and preserve the ancient Vedic traditions.

His teachings emphasized the unity of all religions, the importance of self-realization, and service to humanity as the highest form of worship. He composed several devotional hymns and wrote commentaries on the Upanishads that continue to inspire spiritual seekers today.

Under his guidance, the temple became not just a place of worship but also a center for learning, with daily classes on scriptures, meditation sessions, and charitable activities for the community.

Even after his mahasamadhi in 1895, his presence is strongly felt in the temple, and his teachings continue to guide the temple's activities and spiritual programs.
